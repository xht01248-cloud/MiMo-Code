# Design Directions Library

Used when the user's brief has no taste-signal and you need to run the 5-Direction Picker (Move 2, Branch B). Also useful as a mental library when you're in Branch A and want to sharpen the assumption you're making.

## How to use the 5-Direction Picker

The point of the picker is not to be exhaustive — it's to force the user into a concrete pick before you burn effort on a direction they'd have rejected.

Rules:
1. **Pick 5 directions that are actually different from each other.** Not five flavors of minimal. Not five color palettes on the same layout. If two options could plausibly share a wireframe, they're not different directions.
2. **Fit the artifact.** A brutalist slide deck is a real option; a brutalist banking dashboard is probably not. Filter the library by "would this actually work for what the user asked for."
3. **Give each direction a name, a mood, a thesis, an audience.**
   - Name: two words max, evocative — "Editorial Restraint," "Neon Grotesque"
   - Mood: three words — "quiet, considered, dense"
   - Thesis: one sentence describing the visual bet
   - Audience: one sentence — who this is designed to land for
4. **Number them 1–5.** Ask the user to reply with a number (or a hybrid, e.g., "1 with the color from 3").

Do not present 5 directions when the brief already has signal. That's overhead; use Junior Designer mode instead.

## The genre library

Twenty directions across five genre clusters. Not exhaustive — a starting palette. Feel free to hybridize.

### Cluster A — Editorial / Considered

**A1. Editorial Restraint.** Long-form magazine typography, generous margins, one accent color used sparingly, real photography or none. References: *The Baffler*, Aeon, Substack (before the redesign), Ramp. Mood: quiet, considered, dense. Best for: brands that want to read as intelligent and non-hype.

**A2. Museum Wall.** Serif display, extreme size contrast (huge headline, small body), plenty of negative space, one artifact photographed head-on. References: MoMA, Rijksmuseum, Are.na. Mood: reverent, curated, still. Best for: cultural institutions, high-end products with real craft.

**A3. Broadsheet.** Newspaper grid, tight leading, hierarchical density, functional color. References: *NYT* Politics section, *Financial Times*, Bloomberg. Mood: authoritative, informational, urgent. Best for: data-heavy, news-adjacent, or serious-analysis brands.

**A4. Journal Modern.** Book-like: chapters, drop caps, page numbers, marginalia. References: *Believer* Magazine, Grafik, Type Directors Club. Mood: literary, patient, made-by-hand. Best for: personal or writer-driven brands.

### Cluster B — Refined Minimal

**B1. Swiss Precision.** Grid-perfect, sans-serif (Neue Haas / Helvetica variant), primary colors used as accents, no illustration. References: Vitsœ, Braun, Massimo Vignelli's NYC subway map, Linear. Mood: precise, quiet, systematic. Best for: tools, developer products, high-function interfaces.

**B2. Apple Landing.** Enormous product hero, spacious dark or light background, sans-serif headline (SF Pro / Inter), soft-lit product shot, minimal chrome. References: apple.com, Vercel, Arc Browser. Mood: aspirational, weightless, premium. Best for: consumer premium products.

**B3. New Enterprise Neutral.** Muted palette, restrained illustration, one gradient permitted at most, corporate but not stiff. References: Stripe, Notion (early), Figma marketing. Mood: capable, trustworthy, calm. Best for: B2B SaaS wanting to feel adult.

**B4. Bauhaus Revival.** Primary colors, geometric shapes, sans-serif tightly kerned, function-forward. References: original Bauhaus posters, current Kickstarter identity, Bloomberg Weekend. Mood: playful within a discipline, foundational. Best for: brands that want to feel principled but not sterile.

### Cluster C — Playful / Expressive

**C1. Kinetic Grotesque.** Big display type, oversized characters that break the grid, saturated color blocks, motion-forward. References: Deezer, Off-White, current MoMA Design Store. Mood: loud, current, in-your-face. Best for: brands going for cultural energy.

**C2. Sunday Comics.** Bright flat color, hand-drawn illustration, informal type pairings, warm register. References: Duolingo, Headspace, Reddit rebrand. Mood: friendly, human, approachable. Best for: consumer apps aimed at broad audiences.

**C3. Y2K Nostalgia.** Chrome gradients, pixel typography accents, aqua & lime, transparency effects, deliberate cheesiness. References: recent Nike SB, Balenciaga runway invites, Sonic 3 marketing. Mood: playful, referential, sharp. Best for: youth brands, fashion, culture products.

**C4. Cartoon Confidence.** Illustrated mascot, hand-lettered headlines, saturated warm palette, exaggerated shapes. References: Mailchimp (peak), Slack (peak), Discord. Mood: warm, brand-forward, approachable. Best for: consumer software with a personality bet.

### Cluster D — Structural / Brutalist

**D1. Web Brutalism.** System font, no visual hierarchy tricks, exposed structure, obvious grid lines or none at all. References: Bloomberg Businessweek, Balenciaga.com, Craigslist (accidentally). Mood: confrontational, unpretentious, dense. Best for: fashion, arts, or brands going hard against slick.

**D2. Terminal.** Monospace, black background or paper white, ASCII structural devices (`---`, `>`, `[ ]`), no images. References: developer tool marketing (Ghostty, Warp), Vercel dashboard, hackernews. Mood: technical, direct, insider. Best for: developer tools, technical brands, IRC-culture-adjacent products.

**D3. Concrete.** Heavy sans-serif in one weight only, off-white background, hairline dividers, no color at all. References: Kinfolk (original), certain arch/design agency sites, Aesop packaging. Mood: austere, physical, honest. Best for: high-end services and premium physical products.

**D4. Zine.** Photocopy texture, hand-drawn arrows, snippet layouts, mixed typefaces, deliberate imperfection. References: Are.na blog, Post Wave, current indie music merch. Mood: personal, unpolished, alive. Best for: cultural, indie, or founder-forward brands.

### Cluster E — Textural / Material

**E1. Analog Warmth.** Cream backgrounds, warm neutrals, film-photo grain, serif type, muted accents. References: Everlane (early), Kinfolk, Aesop. Mood: warm, patient, made. Best for: consumer premium, wellness, hospitality.

**E2. Botanical / Editorial Illustration.** Detailed illustrated elements (botanical, cartographic), serif-with-swash display, muted greens and terracottas. References: Rifle Paper Co, Herman Miller, The Sill. Mood: intentional, artisanal, considered. Best for: goods, home, wellness.

**E3. Layered Print.** Halftone dots, overprint color effects, offset registration, textured paper feel even on screen. References: recent NYT Cooking, Rare Beauty packaging, Poster House museum. Mood: rich, hand-made, dimensional. Best for: brands that want to feel like they belong on a shelf.

**E4. Retro Editorial.** 70s-referencing muted colors, condensed sans-serif headlines, tight leading, thin dividers. References: *Wired* (early 2000s), Wax Nine Journal, current Standard Hotel campaigns. Mood: knowing, textured, culturally-literate. Best for: culture, fashion, media.

## How to hybridize (if the picker doesn't quite fit)

Most real briefs don't land squarely in one cell of this grid. Common productive hybrids:

- **Editorial Restraint × Terminal.** A quiet magazine-typography brand for a developer tool. See: recent Cloudflare marketing.
- **Swiss Precision × Kinetic Grotesque.** Rigor + one loud typographic move per page. See: Linear's landing page.
- **Analog Warmth × Museum Wall.** Curated, warm, considered. Slow. See: high-end restaurant identities.
- **Web Brutalism × Broadsheet.** Dense structure, no polish, information-forward. See: FT Alphaville, Bloomberg dashboards.
- **Cartoon Confidence × Bauhaus Revival.** Playful but principled. See: Notion (peak), Duolingo (recent).

When you offer a hybrid in the picker, name both parents in the direction name — "Editorial Restraint × Terminal" — so the user can see the ancestry.

## What to avoid in the picker itself

- **Do not include a "safe default" direction** as one of your five. If four are interesting and one is "clean modern professional SaaS," the user will pick the safe one, and you'll have wasted the exercise. Make all five real bets.
- **Do not describe directions in adjectives only.** Reference at least one real brand per direction. The reference is what makes the direction concrete.
- **Do not include a direction the brief rules out.** If the user said "banking product," don't waste a slot on Zine or Y2K.
